8 nov 2023

game design article response

Reading Amir Dori's game design UI best practices guide made me uncomfortable because it showed the thought process of a profit maximalist - e.g. design with the objective of maximizing revenue rather than accomplishing user good. For instance, almost every bit of design research Dori has done is framed in how it can improve monetization or retaining attention. Store menus are set up to make buying things more convenient than backing out; menus are laid out to force users to accidentally click on advertising, using UX tricks to skirt around usability rules.

Unfortunately, despite all of the posturing about "making the world a better place," this seems to be the norm for modern UI / UX design, something which has turned me off of the field completely. Modern designers aren't taught how to solve important problems, they're taught how to manipulate users into achieving goals that further the capital of the corporations they work for, even if this work is harmful to consumers, children, or human rights. Even student-run clubs like Design Interactive or DCC are setup to place frankly evil companies like Amazon or big oil on pedestals, advocating for personal career success or wealth at all costs.

I really hope we can change the narrative of the design field to place ethical responsibility of our design choices back in the center, instead of giving platforms to designers like Dori.

25 oct 2023

deeper narratives through images

The NYT article about using images to facilitate interest and closer reading among students really resonated with me as I have also experienced the difficulty of being forced to read or analyze subjects I am not interested in. At the same time, there is simply so much content out there that it is difficult to even identify what is worth reading.

I think images can be highly helpful in bridging this gap as they can catch attention and inspire audiences to look closer without wasting their time. Especially when combined with interactive sites or displays, they can be a great tool. Learning how to more closely analyze images and ask questions as described in the article is a vital skill not just to our own research but also to make considerations when designing websites with images.

I recently came across this website which I really loved. The use images and visuals in a very bold intriguing way that kind of forces you to read in deeper.

23 oct 2023

overlays design pattern research

I read "Overuse of Overlays" by the Nielson Norman Group which expanded on some common problems with overlay misuse. The basic premise is that designers need to be more thoughtful about how they incorporate overlays, asking themselves "Who", "What", "When", "Where", and "Why?"

For instance, a user should fully understand how to interface with the overlay and its presence should not be disruptive to their goals. Overlays that intrude on the browsing experience unprompted, or whose controls are ambiguous or confusing, create negative experiences. Another consideration is that overlays must be built to work properly on all screen sizes and be vetted for bugs so that they don't completely wall off the entire use of the website.

I will be sure to consider these points the next time I think about incorporating an overlay!

16 oct 2023

research on form design

Forms are important but can also be some of the most frustrating parts of a web experience, especially since nearly every form is built differently. In order to make a form as accessible and effective as possible, it should follow some rules:

  • reduce the amount of inputs
  • use basic top-down one-column hierarchy
  • split the form into multiple steps to prevent overwhelming the user
  • use intuitive labels and indicate required fields
  • explain formatting requirements
  • reduce clicks and other friction

One example of a website which I think has very intuitive forms is the getCalFresh website. The design of the form makes it so much easier for people in need to submit their information for aid. In addition to having an effective UI, it only asks a few questions at a time, evaluating what information and what questions to ask next to reduce unnecessary steps and make things as simple as possible.
